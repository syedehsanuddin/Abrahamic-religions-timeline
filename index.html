Pathways to Faith

Tracing the Lineage from Prophet Adam to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam


Explore the journey of faith through time, from the origins of humanity with Prophet Adam to the emergence of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. Uncover the key milestones and lineages that have shaped our world's major religions

Prophet Adam



• Islam: In Islam, Adam is considered the first prophet and the first human being created by Allah. He and his wife, Hawwa (Eve), were the first humans and were placed in the Garden of Eden. They are regarded as the parents of all humanity.

• Judaism: While the Hebrew Bible does not explicitly refer to Adam as a prophet, he is considered the first human being created by God and plays a significant role in Jewish creation narratives.

• Christianity: Adam and Eve are central figures in Christian theology. They are seen as the first humans created by God and are known for the story of the Fall in the Garden of Eden, which is a fundamental concept in Christian doctrine.

3000-2800 BCE

Idris (Enoch)

2,600 - 2300 BCE

Nuh (Noah)

• Islam: In Islam, Noah is considered a prophet who was chosen to warn his people about the impending flood as a punishment for their disbelief. He is seen as a messenger of God and a symbol of patience and faith.

• Judaism: In Judaism, Noah is a key figure, and the story of the Great Flood is found in the Torah (the first five books of the Hebrew Bible). He is seen as a righteous man chosen by God to build an ark and save humanity and animals from the flood.

• Christianity: The story of Noah and the Ark is also present in the Christian Bible, specifically in the Book of Genesis. Noah is regarded as a righteous man and a central character in the history of God's covenant with humanity.

2000-1800 BCE

Ibrahim (Abraham)

• Islam: In Islam, Abraham is considered a prominent prophet who played a significant role in the construction of the Kaaba in Mecca and is known for his unwavering faith in one God (Allah). He is regarded as the father of the Abrahamic faiths.

• Judaism: Abraham is a pivotal figure in Judaism, often referred to as the "father of the Jewish people." God's covenant with Abraham is a central theme, and he is known for his willingness to sacrifice his son, Isaac, as a test of faith.

• Christianity: Abraham is also highly regarded in Christianity, primarily as a model of faith. The Christian New Testament references him as a person of great faith and a spiritual ancestor of believers.

1800-1700 BCE

Ishmael

• Islam: In Islam, Ishmael (Ismail) is recognized as a prophet and the firstborn son of the prophet Abraham (Ibrahim). According to Islamic tradition, Ishmael and his mother Hagar were left in the desolate Arabian desert, where they settled in what would become Mecca. Ishmael is seen as a virtuous figure who played a role in the construction of the Kaaba, one of Islam's holiest sites.

• Judaism: In Judaism, Ishmael is also considered the firstborn son of Abraham but is not regarded as a prophet. His story is primarily associated with the rivalry between him and Isaac, as both were seen as heirs to God's promises to Abraham. Ishmael and Hagar were eventually cast out by Sarah, leading to their settlement in the wilderness.

• Christianity: Ishmael is mentioned in the Old Testament but does not hold the same level of significance as he does in Islam. His story is primarily viewed in the context of the broader narrative of Abraham's family.


Ishaq

• Islam: In Islam, Isaac (Ishaq) is considered a prophet and the son of the prophet Abraham (Ibrahim). The Quran mentions Isaac and his birth as a miracle, as he was born to his mother Sarah in her old age. He is also recognized for his role in the lineage of the Prophets, as many of his descendants became prophets. Isaac's story is intertwined with that of his father Abraham, and he is seen as a righteous figure.

• Judaism: In Judaism, Isaac is a central figure and is often referred to as one of the patriarchs. He is the son of Abraham and Sarah and the father of Jacob (Israel). The story of the binding of Isaac (the Akedah), in which God tested Abraham's faith by instructing him to sacrifice his son, is a significant event in Jewish tradition.

• Christianity: Isaac is also a notable figure in Christianity, especially in the Old Testament. The story of the binding of Isaac is present in the Christian Bible as well. He is seen as a key figure in the lineage leading to Jesus, though the focus of Christian theology shifts to Jesus as the Messiah.


1700-1600 BCE

Jacob (Ya'qub)

• Islam: In Islam, Jacob (Ya'qub) is considered a prophet and a righteous figure. He is regarded as the son of Isaac (Ishaq) and the grandson of Abraham (Ibrahim). Jacob is often mentioned alongside his father and grandfather as part of the lineage of prophets. His story is mentioned in the Quran, particularly in relation to his sons, including Joseph (Yusuf), who is a prominent figure in Islamic tradition.

• Judaism: In Judaism, Jacob is a central patriarch and one of the forefathers of the twelve tribes of Israel. He is known for his significant role in the Abrahamic covenant and for fathering twelve sons who would become the leaders of the twelve tribes of Israel. Jacob's life is detailed in the Book of Genesis, including the story of his wrestling with an angel and the blessings he bestowed upon his sons.

• Christianity: In Christianity, Jacob is also recognized as one of the patriarchs, particularly in the Old Testament. His life and the twelve tribes of Israel play a crucial role in the biblical narrative, although the primary focus of Christian theology shifts to Jesus as the central figure, and Jacob's significance is more in terms of lineage.


Jacob's story and the blessings he conferred upon his sons are particularly significant in the traditions of both Judaism and Christianity. In Islam, while he is a prophet, his story is not as extensively detailed in the Quran as in the Hebrew Bible and the Old Testament.

1300-1200 BCE

Moses (Musa)

• Islam: In Islam, Moses (Musa) is considered one of the most important prophets and is often referred to as "Kaleemullah," the one to whom God spoke directly. He is known for leading the Israelites out of slavery in Egypt and receiving the Ten Commandments on Mount Sinai. His story is recounted in the Quran, and he is highly respected for his leadership, guidance, and role as a lawgiver.

• Judaism: In Judaism, Moses is a central figure and is often regarded as the greatest prophet and leader of the Israelites. He is credited with leading the Israelites out of Egypt, receiving the Torah (the first five books of the Hebrew Bible) on Mount Sinai, and establishing the Mosaic Law, which includes the Ten Commandments.

• Christianity: Moses is also a significant figure in Christianity, particularly in the Old Testament. His role in leading the Israelites and receiving the Ten Commandments is recognized, and he is seen as a precursor to Jesus. In the New Testament, Moses is mentioned, but the focus shifts to Jesus as the central figure.

1040-931 BCE

Solomon (Sulaiman)

• Islam: In Islamic tradition, Solomon (Sulaiman) is considered a prophet and a king known for his wisdom and ability to communicate with animals. He is credited with various achievements, including the construction of the First Temple in Jerusalem. His story is mentioned in the Quran, highlighting his just rule and the miracles he performed.

• Judaism: In Jewish tradition, Solomon is a prominent figure and is known for his wisdom. He is traditionally attributed to the construction of the First Temple in Jerusalem and is associated with many proverbs and wise sayings found in the Book of Proverbs.

• Christianity: Solomon is acknowledged in Christian tradition, primarily in the Old Testament, where his wisdom and reign are described. In the New Testament, Jesus refers to Solomon as a symbol of wisdom, but the focus of Christian theology is on Jesus as the Messiah.

1040-931 BCE

David (Dawud)

• Islam: In Islam, David (Dawud) is considered a prophet and a king. He is highly regarded for his wisdom, leadership, and his ability to receive divine revelations through the Psalms (Zabur). The Quran mentions David as a prophet who was granted great power and authority by Allah. He is often portrayed as a just ruler and a symbol of devotion to God.

• Judaism: In Judaism, David is a central figure, best known for becoming the second king of the United Kingdom of Israel, following King Saul. He is revered as a great warrior, musician, and poet. David is traditionally credited with composing many of the Psalms found in the Hebrew Bible. His reign is considered a golden era in Israel's history, and he is also an ancestor of the Messiah in Jewish belief.

• Christianity: In Christianity, David is significant in the Old Testament and is known as a righteous king. He is viewed as a precursor to Jesus, the promised Messiah, who is often referred to as the "Son of David." The genealogy of Jesus is traced back to David in the New Testament.

4 BCE-30 CE

Jesus (Isa)

• Islam: In Islam, Jesus (Isa) is considered a prophet and not divine. He is born to the Virgin Mary and is highly revered as a righteous prophet who performed miracles and preached monotheism. However, he is not seen as the Son of God but rather as a messenger of God.

• Christianity: In Christianity, Jesus is the central figure and is regarded as the Son of God, the Messiah, and the savior of humanity. Christians believe in the Holy Trinity, with Jesus being one of the three persons of the Godhead.

570 CE

Muhammad (P.B.U.H)
